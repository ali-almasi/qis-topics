Topics in Quantum Information Science

Quantum Singular Value Transformation (QSVT)

Introduction

This algorithm is part of the grand unification program, which aims to find out what the common structure is between the different quantum algorithms. Having such a unification that all quantum algorithms developed so far can be seen as special cases of it, we may be able to optimize the quantum algorithms and develop new ones more easily.

The claim is that all (?) quantum algorithms can be seen as polynomial transformations of the singular values of a linear operator (embedded in a unitary). With this in mind, we can expect that transforming with the optimal polynomial will allow us to answer specific questions about the singular values, hopefully with a speedup.

Preleminaries

Parity of Polynomials

Let \(P\) be a polynomial. We call \(P\) even if \(P(-x) = P(x)\) for all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), and odd if \(P(-x) = -P(x)\) for all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\). The even part of \(P\) is denoted by \(P_e = \frac{P(x) + P(-x)}{2}\) and the odd part by \(P_o = \frac{P(x) - P(-x)}{2}\).

Rotations around the Pauli Axes

Consider the Pauli matrices \[ X = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad Y = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad Z = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}. \] One can show that any \(2 \times 2\) density matrix can be written as \[ \rho = \frac{1}{2} (I + r_x X + r_y Y + r_z Z), \] where \((r_x, r_y, r_z)\) is a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) satisfying \(\| r \| \leq 1\). Now, consider applying the unitary \(e^{i \theta X} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & i\sin(\theta) \\ i\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix}\) to the state \(\rho\). Let us compute each term of \(\rho' = e^{i \theta X} \rho e^{-i \theta X}\) separately:

\[\begin{align} e^{i \theta X} X e^{-i \theta X} &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & i\sin(\theta) \\ i\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -i\sin(\theta) \\ -i\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} = X,\\ e^{i \theta X} Y e^{-i \theta X} &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & i\sin(\theta) \\ i\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -i\sin(\theta) \\ -i\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} = - \sin(2\theta) Z + \cos(2\theta) Y,\\ e^{i \theta X} Z e^{-i \theta X} &= \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & i\sin(\theta) \\ i\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -i\sin(\theta) \\ -i\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} = \cos(2\theta) Z + \sin(2\theta) Y. \end{align}\]

We see that the resulting state has coordinates \[(r_x, r_y(\cos(2\theta)+\sin(2\theta)), r_z(\cos(2\theta)-\sin(2\theta))),\] showing that the transformation is a rotation of angle \(-2\theta\) around the \(X\) axis in the Bloch sphere.

With a similar reasoning, we can deduce the following:

Proposition 1.1 The following gates are rotations with angle \(-2\theta\) around the corresponding axes in the Bloch sphere: \[ e^{i \theta X} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & i\sin(\theta) \\ i\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix}, \quad e^{i \theta Y} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & \sin(\theta) \\ -\sin(\theta) & \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix}, \quad e^{i \theta Z} = \begin{bmatrix} e^{i\theta} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-i\theta} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Quantum Signal Processing

This framework was introduced by @LYC16. The problem statement is as follows:

Consider having access to the following single-qubit unitaries:

  • a signal gate \[ W(a) = \begin{bmatrix} a & i\sqrt{1-a^2} \\ i\sqrt{1-a^2} & a \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta/2) & i \sin(\theta/2) \\ i \sin(\theta/2) & \cos(\theta/2) \end{bmatrix}, \] where \(a \in [-1,1]\) and \(\theta = - 2 \arccos(a)\),

  • a family of tunable signal processing gates \[ S(\phi) = e^{i\phi Z} = \begin{bmatrix} e^{i\phi} & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-i\phi} \end{bmatrix} = e^{i\phi} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & e^{-2i\phi} \end{bmatrix}.^{Which roation is this one?} \]

Suppose then we can apply any sequence of unitaries of the form \[ U_{\vec{\phi}} = S(\phi_0) \Pi_{i = 1}^{d} W(a) S(\phi_i), \] where \(a\) is unknown but the vector \(\vec{\phi} = (\phi_0, \ldots, \phi_d)\) can be arbitrarily chosen. Which unitaries can we obtain this way?

Theorem 1.1 (Quantum Signal Processing) Let \(\vec{\phi} = (\phi_0, \ldots, \phi_d) \in \mathbb{R}^{d+1}\). Then there exist \(P , Q \in \mathbb{C}[a]\) such that for all \(a \in [-1,1]\), \[ U_{\vec{\phi}} = \begin{bmatrix} P(a) & i Q(a) \sqrt{1 - a^2} \\ i Q^*(a) \sqrt{1 - a^2} & P^*(a) \end{bmatrix} \] and

  1. \(\operatorname{deg}(P) \leq d\) and \(\operatorname{deg}(Q) \leq d - 1\),
  2. \(P\) has parity \(d \mod 2\) and \(Q\) has parity \((d - 1) \mod 2\),
  3. for all \(a \in [-1,1]\), \(| P(a) |^2 + (1 - a^2) | Q(a) |^2 = 1\). Moreover, if \(P , Q \in \mathbb{C}[a]\) satisfy the above conditions, then there exists \(\vec{\phi} \in \mathbb{R}^{d+1}\) such that \(U_{\vec{\phi}}\) is of the form above.

As stated by @grandUnif21, we may be interested in the achivable polynomial transformation of the input on a subsystem. Let us choose

\[\operatorname{Poly}(a) = \langle + | U_{\vec{\phi}} | + \rangle = \operatorname{Re}(P(a)) + i \operatorname{Re}(Q(a)) \sqrt{1 - a^2}. \]

Then, we can prove that \(\vec{\phi}\) can be chosen such that \(\langle + | U_{\vec{\phi}} | + \rangle\) approximates any real polynomial \(\operatorname{Poly}\) with

  1. \(\operatorname{Poly}\) has parity \(d \mod 2\),
  2. \(\operatorname{deg}(\operatorname{Poly}) \leq d\),
  3. \(\| \operatorname{Poly}(a) \|\leq 1\) for all \(a \in [-1,1]\).

Amplitude Amplification

Assume that we have a quantum circuit \(A\) that acts on \(m\) qubits in the way that \[ A | 0^m \rangle = \sqrt{p} | G \rangle + \sqrt{1 - p} | B \rangle, \] where \(| G \rangle\) is for some reason a good state for us that we want to amplify its weight, and \(| B \rangle\) is a bad state. We also assume that \(\langle G | B \rangle = 0\).

Amplitude Amplification via QSP

The problem statement is as follows: given \(U\) and \(U^{\dagger}\), such that \(\langle A_0 | U | B_0 \rangle \neq 0\), and operators \(A_\phi = e^{i \phi | A_0 \rangle \langle A_0 |}\) and \(B_\phi = e^{i \phi | B_0 \rangle \langle B_0 |}\), and we want to build a gate \(Q\) such that \[ | \langle A_0 | Q | B_0 \rangle \rightarrow 1 \].

Let us define \(| A_{\perp} \rangle\) and \(| B_{\perp} \rangle\) as

  • \(U | B_0 \rangle = a | A_0 \rangle + \sqrt{1 - a^2} | A_{\perp} \rangle\),
  • \(U | B_{\perp} \rangle = \sqrt{1 - a^2} | A_0 \rangle - a | A_{\perp} \rangle\).

Then, as long as we prepare a \(U^{\dagger}\) after \(U\), after a \(U^{\dagger}\) , etc, we are mapping from \(\operatorname{Span} \{ | B_0 \rangle, | B_{\perp} \rangle \}\) to \(\operatorname{Span} \{ | A_0 \rangle, | A_{\perp} \rangle \}\), and back.

Thus we are essentially working with a qubit.

Theorem 1.2 (Amplitude Amplification) \[ \langle A_0 | \Pi_{i = 1}^{d/2} U B_{\phi_{2i-1}} U^{\dagger} A_{\phi_{2i}} | B_0 \rangle = \operatorname{Poly}(a) \] with \(\operatorname{Poly}\) satisfying the conditions of the QSP theorem.

Having the above, all we need is to find \(\operatorname{Poly}(a)\) such that \(| \operatorname{Poly}(a) |\) gets as close to 1 as possible.

Remark. This can be done in an oblivious way, i.e., without knowing the value of \(a\).
